How we work

Three things we believe

AI won't replace you — only the excuses

We're not professional developers — we're users learning, in real time, what AI makes possible. The fear is natural: we fear what we don't understand. But there's only one way to understand AI — use it to do something you didn't think you could. It won't replace you; it replaces the excuses we've all grown tired of: I'm not technical, that's not my job, there's no time, IT would never approve it. Judgment and taste stay yours; synthesis and scale are on tap. We're on the same journey you are.

Good UX should be the default, not a privilege

Bad defaults aren't a technology problem — they're a UX problem, and they're fixable. The friction you hit every day isn't inevitable; it persists because the people who own the platform rarely feel it. They ship the roadmap; you live with the gaps. We refuse to treat that as the natural order — so we close the gap two ways: recommend the tool that already gets it right, or build the fix that doesn't exist yet. Either way, good UX stops being a privilege you hope trickles down.

The browser is the runtime, you stay in control

Everything we build and everything we recommend runs on your machine, in your browser — no server-side dependency, no telemetry, no account to create, no agenda beyond the craft. That's not a feature, it's the floor: your data never leaves, there's nothing phoning home, and nothing anyone can quietly switch off or paywall later. Even the fixes you build with AI land here, in your hands — not in someone else's cloud.

What we vet

We vet what already works.

When an app or tool already delivers good UX, we don't rebuild it — we run it through a written checklist and tell you exactly why it earned the recommendation. Every entry carries a Spark-Vetted seal with a grade and a re-review date.

Vetted doesn't mean perfect — it means an honest place to start. Every grade names the caveats out loud, because most of what we recommend is good enough to use and still has something you'd want fixed.

What we build

We build what's missing.

Often "missing" starts inside something we already vetted. Google Workspace is a fine place to start — but it gives you no clean way to drop an app launcher into an intranet page and get people to the right tool fast. So we built Gapps Embed. That's the pattern: a vetted tool gets you most of the way, and you build the last mile. (SNapp Extension is in rebrand; more are queued.)

A vetted tool you'd use if only one thing were fixed isn't a dead end — it's where the next build starts.

Why now

This is the unlock.

For the first time, the tools to fix bad software are in your hands — and a lot of that capability is free. The same AI that helps you draft an email can help you build the fix you've been waiting years for someone else to ship. Alone, that solves your friction. But the friction you live with, thousands of others live with too.

So band together. Pool the capacity, share the fixes, vet them against a real bar — and we, the users, out-build the gatekeepers, for everyone. One person fixing one annoyance is a workaround; a thousand of us sharing the fixes is a shift in who software gets built for. That instinct nagging at you about a tool that should be better is the raw material. The only thing this needs is for you to show up.

By users, for users.

You've always been told your feedback matters. And maybe it does — but you were still one voice against priorities that were never yours, whether the gatekeeper was a vendor, an IT department, or a backlog no one staffs. Here's what's actually different: we don't promise to listen better. The equalizer is AI done right — you stop waiting to be heard and start holding the lever. You badger the model until it gets the fix right. (It might even cop an attitude with you. Get it right anyway.) That's not a support queue you're stuck in — it's a tool in your hands. Don't file a ticket. Rewrite the logic.
